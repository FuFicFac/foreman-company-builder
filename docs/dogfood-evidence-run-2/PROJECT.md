# Project Brief: Short-Story Commission

## Title
**The Tuesday Ledger**

## Premise
In a mid-size industrial city, a municipal records clerk discovers that three consecutive “accidental” water-main breaks share a signature only someone who files the work orders would notice. When the pattern points at her own department—and at the colleague who trained her—she has one week of paper trail before the archive is digitized and the originals are shredded. The story is not a thriller chase; it is about what a careful person does with proof she never asked to hold.

## Characters
- **Elena Voss** — Protagonist; 34; assistant clerk in the Department of Public Works records office; meticulous, risk-averse, recently promoted past a senior clerk who expected the job.
- **Ray Okonkwo** — Senior clerk who mentored Elena for six years; genial, indispensable on the floor, the person whose initials appear on the “routine” re-filings that don’t match the field logs.
- **Captain June Park** — City inspector who signed the final safety clearances after each break; appears only in documents and one brief in-person exchange; not a villain cartoon, but her signature is the hinge Elena cannot ignore.

## Timeline
- **March 3, 2025 (Monday)** — First water-main break: Willow & 12th. Official cause: “age of pipe.” Elena files the incident packet same day; notices the repair crew’s timesheet was logged *before* the break call-in time, but shrugs it off as a clock error.
- **March 17, 2025 (Monday)** — Second break: Mercer Industrial Spur. Same “age of pipe” language; same pre-stamped timesheet pattern. Elena begins a private notebook (paper only).
- **March 31, 2025 (Monday)** — Third break: under the loading dock of the city warehouse that stores surplus copper fittings. Elena matches three work-order numbers to a single re-file batch Ray initialed on **April 1**.
- **April 1–4, 2025** — Digitization vendor on-site; originals marked for shredding after scan. Elena has access to the pre-shred room only during her shifts: **8:00 a.m.–4:30 p.m.**, Monday–Friday.
- **April 7, 2025 (Monday), 3:55 p.m.** — Story’s present-day climax window: last chance to pull the three physical packets before the vendor seals the bin. Elena’s shift ends at 4:30; the shred truck is scheduled for **4:15 p.m.**
- Continuity rules for the writer: all three breaks land on Mondays exactly two weeks apart; the re-file batch date is the day *after* the third break; Elena’s notebook entries must not invent events outside this span; no “months later” epilogue.

## Required beats
1. Open in the records office on **April 7** with Elena mid-routine, then flash back only as needed—and only within the March 3–April 7 window—to establish how she noticed the pattern.
2. Show the documentary evidence on the page (timesheets, re-file initials, Park’s clearances) so the reader can follow without trusting Elena’s paranoia alone.
3. A live friction scene with Ray (not a confession dump): he is helpful, almost kind, and that makes the choice harder.
4. One concrete obstacle to removing the files (badge log, co-worker present, vendor early, locked cart—pick one and make it physical).
5. End on Elena’s choice in the room, before 4:15, without a narrator verdict on whether she was right.

## Constraints
- **Length:** 600–900 words.
- **Voice:** Third person, past tense.
- **POV:** Single — Elena only. No head-hopping; Ray and Park only as Elena perceives them.
- No lighthouse, lamp, or characters named Theo or Mara.
- No omniscient reveal of a grand conspiracy past what Elena can prove from paper and one conversation.
- Keep municipal detail specific enough to feel real; avoid procedural lecture.

## Ending
The story must close on a **concrete choice Elena makes on the page** at or just before the shred deadline—e.g., she either walks the three packets out under her coat, feeds them into the shredder herself, slides them into Ray’s pending tray, or hands them to Park in the lobby. The last beat is the action of choosing, not the long-term consequence.
